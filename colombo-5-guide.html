The complete guide to Colombo 5.

A neighborhood worth slowing down for. Where to eat, what to do, where to stay, and how to spend a day.

Colombo 5 is one of fifteen postal districts in the city of Colombo, Sri Lanka. It is shaped roughly like a leaf, bordered by Havelock Road to the west, Baseline Road to the east, the Wellawatte canal to the south, and the edges of Bambalapitiya to the north. The postcode is 00500. Most of what makes it worth knowing about, however, has very little to do with any of that.

We have lived in Colombo 5, on Kirulapone Avenue specifically, for ten years. What follows is what we have learned by walking it, eating in it, sending guests through it, and waking up in it five thousand mornings in a row.

It is not a complete guide. There is no such thing. But it is the guide we would write if a friend were moving in and asked us what to know.

Where Colombo 5 sits in the city

Colombo is divided into postcodes — Colombo 1 through Colombo 15 — and each postcode has its own character. Colombo 5 is a residential district about six kilometers south of the central business hub at Colombo 1 (Fort) and Colombo 2 (Slave Island), and just inland of the seafront neighborhoods of Bambalapitiya (Colombo 4) and Wellawatte (Colombo 6).

It is not a tourist district. It does not have the colonial-era hotels of Colombo 7 (Cinnamon Gardens) or the seafront promenade of Colombo 3 (Kollupitiya). What it has, instead, are tree-lined lanes, mid-century houses that have not been knocked down, a market that opens at dawn, three serious bakeries, and the kind of walking pace that has gone missing in most of central Colombo.

If you want a stay that feels like a city, stay in Colombo 3. If you want one that feels like a house, stay in Colombo 5.

The lanes within the postcode

Colombo 5 is small enough to walk and varied enough to reward walking. Four sub-areas worth knowing.

Kirulapone is the residential heart. Quiet lanes, mature trees, small shops, the Kirulapone market, and the temple at the end of the road. This is where we are. It is the part of Colombo 5 that has changed least in ten years.

Havelock Town runs along Havelock Road, the spine of Colombo 5. Busier — supermarkets, banks, the cinema, the larger commercial buildings, restaurants. It is where you go when you need to do errands.

Pamankada is the southern edge, near the canal. More residential, more recent construction. The bridge at Pamankada Junction is a useful landmark.

Thimbirigasyaya is the northern tip, where Colombo 5 meets Colombo 7. Some of the best small restaurants in the city are here, and the streets get prettier the more you look at them.

Where to eat in Colombo 5

A serious answer to this question takes its own article — and we have one coming. For now, the short version.

For a long lunch with rice and curry, our own restaurant — Vinya Garden, in the back of the property — is the easy answer. We have to say it. We also mean it.

For roadside excellence, the kottu place under the tin roof three minutes east of us is among the best in Colombo. Loud, plastic chairs, chicken kottu at one in the afternoon. Don't sit too close to the griddle.

For the bakery on the corner of First Lane — the one with the green sign and the soft butter cake in the window — buy two if you have any sense.

For Sri Lankan home cooking, the small rice and curry place two minutes south of us, no English sign, no menu, eight curries on a banana leaf. The auntie who runs it remembers what you don't eat from the second visit onward.

For coffee that takes itself seriously, there are two cafés in Havelock Town worth the walk. We will list them, with addresses, in the longer guide.

What to do in Colombo 5

Colombo 5 is not a sights-heavy district. It is a living-here district. The best things to do are most of them slow.

Walk. The lanes off Havelock Road — Vihara Lane, First Lane, the back of Kirulapone — are full of houses worth looking at, gardens worth peering into, and the kind of small architecture (verandahs, wooden gates, jacaranda trees) that disappears the further you get from this part of the city.

Sit. Viharamahadevi Park is fifteen minutes by tuk-tuk. The benches are old and worn smooth. Take a book.

Visit the Kirulapone market. It opens at six and runs hot until nine. Mangoes, jackfruit, banana, papaya, pumpkin the size of a child's head. Two of the women on the left side have been selling vegetables in the same spot since before we opened.

Walk the seafront. Wellawatte Beach is a tuk-tuk ride south. The beach itself is for walking, not for swimming. The roti shops along Galle Road are for eating, especially the one with the long queue at five in the afternoon.

Spend an afternoon at the National Museum. Around the corner from Viharamahadevi Park. The bronze Buddha on the ground floor is reason enough to go.

How to get around

Colombo 5 is designed for short journeys. Three modes will cover most of what you need.

Walking. Most of what you will want is within a thirty-minute walk. The lanes are flat, the heat is manageable in the morning and evening, and the things worth seeing reveal themselves slowly.

Tuk-tuks. The default for short hops. Use the PickMe app for fixed prices — no haggling, GPS-tracked, tipping optional. LKR 250 to 500 for most destinations within Colombo 5. LKR 600 to 1,000 to nearby districts.

Buses. The 138 bus runs from Fort to Maharagama and stops on Havelock Road at the corner of our lane. LKR 30 in either direction. The 100 and 101 also run useful routes through Havelock Road. Bus travel takes longer, but it lets you see the city.

For longer journeys, PickMe Cars or Uber are the standard. Trains run to and from Colombo Fort and along the coastal line; the nearest stations to us are Kirulapone (five minutes' walk) and Bambalapitiya (twenty minutes by tuk-tuk).

Where to stay in Colombo 5

Colombo 5 has a small but growing collection of boutique hotels, guesthouses, and serviced apartments. Most travelers underestimate the difference between staying in a residential district and staying in a hotel zone — the first feels like a stay; the second feels like a transit point.

We will not pretend not to have a preference. We run a small hotel — six named rooms, a garden, indoor and outdoor seating spread across the property — and we built it for travelers who want a city stay that feels like a house. If that sounds right, you can read about the house or book a room. When to come

Colombo's weather is broadly two seasons: dry (December to March) and wet (May to October). November and April are transitional and unpredictable.

December through February is the most comfortable time — cooler nights, manageable days, low humidity. March and April are the hottest. May through August is the southwest monsoon — heavy afternoon rains, often beautiful mornings. October has the second monsoon and the heaviest of the year's storms.

For Colombo 5 specifically, the dry months are also the busiest socially. The garden parties, the food markets, the cultural calendar — all of it lives in the December-to-February window.

A longer guide to seasonal travel — what to pack, what to avoid, what is open when — is in the forthcoming Best Time to Visit Sri Lanka.

A day in Colombo 5

If we had a day to spend in Colombo 5 — not on errands, not on work, just on the place — we would do this.

Six in the morning: walk to the Kirulapone market. Buy mangoes. Drink coffee from the small kade across the road.

Eight: breakfast somewhere with kiribath. Our garden, if we are honest.

Ten: walk the residential lanes around Vihara Road and First Lane. Look at the houses. Stop at the bakery on the corner.

Noon: rice and curry at the auntie's place. Eight curries, banana leaf, no menu.

Two: rest in the heat of the day. Sit. Read. Lie down.

Four: tuk-tuk to Viharamahadevi Park. Walk slowly under the rain trees. The National Museum is fifteen minutes' walk if you have energy.

Six: kottu at the place under the tin roof. Listen to the chopping. Eat fast, walk slow.

Eight: a quiet drink in our garden, or a louder one at the bar around the corner.

Ten: home.

This is not a complete day. It is a quiet one. We have done it many times.

Common questions

What is Colombo 5 known for?
Quiet residential lanes, mid-century houses, the Kirulapone market, the corridor of restaurants and shops along Havelock Road, and a handful of boutique hotels that escape the busier tourist districts.

Is Colombo 5 a good area for tourists?
For travelers who want a slower, residential stay close to the city center, yes. It is not a hotel district in the conventional sense — the appeal is precisely that it is not.

Is Colombo 5 safe?
Yes. Colombo 5 is one of the quieter and safer residential districts of the city. The usual urban precautions apply, and otherwise it is a district where people walk to the shops, send their children to school, and live ordinary lives.

How far is Colombo 5 from the airport?
About 32 kilometers south. By car, 45 to 90 minutes depending on traffic. The full guide: How to Get from Bandaranaike Airport to Colombo 5.

How far is Colombo 5 from Colombo Fort?
About 5 kilometers, or 15 to 25 minutes by tuk-tuk. The 138 bus runs the route directly along Havelock Road.

What is the postal code for Colombo 5?
00500.
